Parts of Speech

1. Noun

Name of people, places, things, or ideas.

  • Proper vs Common: Ali (Proper) vs A man (Common)
  • Countable vs Uncountable: Flowers (Countable) vs Sunlight (Uncountable)
  • Collective: flock, team, group, ...
Proper noun: Paris is beautiful in spring.
Collective noun: The team celebrated their victory.

2. Pronoun

Replace nouns.

  • Personal: I, him, her,...
  • Relative: who, which, where, ...
  • Reflexive: yourself, herself,...
  • Demonstrative: this, those, that,...
  • Indefinite: All, everything, ...
Relative pronoun: The book that I read was fascinating.
Demonstrative pronoun: This is my favorite coffee shop.

3. Adjective

Describes a noun or a pronoun.

The beautiful sunset, a tall building.

4. Verb

Indicate action or state of being.

  • Action verbs: Run, play, ...
  • Linking verbs: is, am,...
  • Auxiliary verbs: can be, should be, must be, ...
Action verb: She dances gracefully.
Linking verb: He is a talented musician.

5. Adverb

Describes a verb, adjective, adverb, or entire sentence.

She ran quickly. (describes verb)
It was extremely hot. (describes adjective)

6. Prepositions

Show time, location,.. (on, under, until, at, etc...).

The book is on the table.
We'll meet at 5 PM.

7. Conjunctions

Link words, phrases, or clauses.

  • Coordinating: FAN BOYS (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So)
  • Subordinating: Although, Because, Since,..
  • Correlative: Neither nor, Either or, etc...
Coordinating: I wanted to go, but it was raining.
Subordinating: Because she studied hard, she passed the exam.

8. Interjections

Express emotions (WOW, OH, etc...).

Wow! That's amazing!
Oh, I didn't know that.

Types of Clauses

1. Independent Clause

A group of words that contains a subject and a verb and convey a clear thought.

She completed her assignment.
The sun is shining brightly.

2. Dependent Clause

A group of words that contains a subject and a verb but cannot stand alone as a complete sentence because it does not express a complete thought.

When she completed her assignment
Because the sun is shining brightly

Types of Sentences

1. Simple Sentence

A sentence that contains a subject and a verb and expresses a single complete thought.

Sam and Alex went to the park and played soccer.

2. Compound Sentence

A sentence that contains two independent clauses joined by a conjunction (FAN BOYS).

It was raining that day, so I didn't go to school.

3. Complex Sentence

A sentence that contains one independent clause, and one or more dependent clauses; these clauses are usually joined by subordinating conjunctions.

After I exercised at the gym, I ate a protein-rich meal.

4. Compound-Complex Sentence

A sentence that contains two independent clauses and at least one dependent clause.

Although the team lost the match, I didn't lose hope in the team, and continued training them as usual.

Punctuation Usage

Punctuation Usage Examples
Period (.) End of imperative or declarative statement, Abbreviations, Ellipses, Inside quotation marks. She went to the store. Dr. Smith arrived.
Quotation (" ") Around the exact speaker's words, Around titles of poems, essays, articles, ..., Use single quotes within double ones. He said, "I'll be there soon."
Question (?) End of a direct question, Inside parentheses, Inside quotation marks. Where are you going? "Did he arrive?" she asked.
Semicolon (;) Instead of a comma and a conjunction, Instead of a period, Before a conjunctive adverb, In lists within a list. I have a test tomorrow; I need to study.
Colon (:) Before a list or series, Introducing a quotation, Salutations in formal emails, Before an appositive, explanation, or example that follows a complete sentence, Between main clauses when the second is an answer or a definition of the other, In ratios, In time. Ingredients: flour, sugar, eggs.
Dash (—) Sudden break in thought, Hesitation, Introduce a summary or explanation, Set information that deserves emphasis. The result—though unexpected—was welcomed.
Comma (,) Before a coordinating conjunction, In lists, After Introductory expressions, After names in direct address, Around appositives, After an introductory dependent clause, Before such as for examples, Around an interrupting expression, Around non-limiting clauses, phrases, or information, With quotations, In dates, After introductory verbal phrases, Around contrasting elements. We bought apples, oranges, and bananas.

Coordination, Subordination, and Parallelism

1. Coordination

Linking clauses of equal importance using conjunctions (FAN BOYS), semicolons between independent clauses, or conjunctive adverbs (therefore, however, consequently,...).

I read a cooking book; however, I still lacked experience.
I studied hard, so I got a perfect score.
I play the violin; it is a very beautiful instrument.

2. Subordination

Highlights the importance of one idea over another by using subordinating conjunctions (Although, Because, Since, If, that, who, which, when ...).

Although he performed well, no one clapped.
I ran back home because my sister fell unconscious.
I will bring you a toy if you act polite.

3. Parallelism

Creating balance in sentences by ensuring consistent grammatical structures in lists, pairs, or series (Gerund, infinitives, ...).

I like to read, to play, and to swim.
Andrea enjoys shopping for new clothes, watching recent movies, and going to the park with Alex.

Run-ons, Comma Splices, and Sentence Fragments

1. Run-on Sentences

A run-on sentence occurs when two or more independent clauses are joined without proper punctuation or a conjunction. We fix these sentences using coordination, subordination, end punctuations, or semicolon.

Incorrect: I love reading it is my favorite hobby.
Correct: I love reading; it is my favorite hobby.
Correct: I love reading because it is my favorite hobby.

2. Comma Splices

A comma splice occurs when two independent clauses are joined by a comma alone. We fix these sentences using same methods as run-ons.

Incorrect: The sky is blue, it is very beautiful.
Correct: The sky is blue; it is very beautiful.
Correct: The sky is blue, and it is very beautiful.

3. Sentence Fragments

A sentence fragment is an incomplete sentence lacking a subject, verb, or complete thought. We fix it by either attaching it to a main clause, or adding the missing subject or verb.

Incorrect: After the meeting ended. We went for lunch.
Correct: After the meeting ended, we went for lunch.
Incorrect: The book on the table.
Correct: The book is on the table.

Inversion

Definition

Inversion refers to the reversal of the usual subject-verb order in a sentence.

Rules

1. Inversion with Negative Adverbs: When a sentence begins with negative adverbs (e.g., never, rarely, seldom, hardly, scarcely, no sooner), the auxiliary verb comes before the subject.

No sooner had she arrived than we sang together.

2. Inversion with 'So + Adjective': When 'so' is followed by an adjective at the start of the sentence, inversion occurs.

So devastating was the destruction due to the blow.

3. Inversion with Adverbial Expressions of Place: When the sentence begins with an adverbial phrase describing location, the verb comes before the subject.

Under the tomb lay a precious treasure.

4. Conditional Inversion: When using third conditional or hypothetical structures, 'if' is omitted and inversion is applied.

Had he studied hard, he would have scored higher.

Tense Consistency

Definition

Tense consistency means maintaining the same grammatical tense (past, present, future) within a sentence, clause, or related group of sentences unless a logical shift in time occurs that justifies a change.

Rules

1. Consistency Within a Sentence: Verbs describing actions or states happening at the same time should share the same tense.

Correct: She walked to the store and bought groceries.
Justified Shift: She walked to the store because she needs milk now.

2. Consistency Within a Paragraph/Narrative: Establish a primary tense (e.g., past tense for storytelling, present tense for general facts/analysis) and stick to it for events occurring in that timeframe.

Yesterday, Maria went to the park. She sat on a bench and read her book. The sun was shining brightly.

3. Shifting Tense for Logical Time Changes: Tense should change when the time frame of the action changes (past, present, future, before, after).

By the time we arrived (Past), the movie had already started (Past Perfect - happened before arrival).

Various Tenses

Verb tenses indicate the time of action/state (past, present, future) and its aspect (simple, continuous, perfect).

Tense Structure Uses Examples
Present Simple Subject + base verb (add -s/-es for 3rd person singular) Habitual actions, General truths, Fixed schedules She teaches Spanish.
Past Simple Subject + past tense verb (regular: -ed; irregular: ate, wrote) Completed past actions, Past habits They visited Paris last summer.
Present Continuous Subject + am/is/are + present participle (-ing) Actions happening now, Temporary situations, Future arrangements The cat is sleeping on the sofa.
Past Continuous Subject + was/were + present participle (-ing) Ongoing past actions that were interrupted, Parallel past actions, Specific time in the past It was raining all morning.
Present Perfect Subject + have/has + past participle Unspecified past actions, Actions continuing to present, Recent actions We have seen this movie twice.
Past Perfect Subject + had + past participle Past-before-past actions, Completed prior actions The train had departed by the time we arrived.
Future Simple Subject + will/shall + base verb Spontaneous decisions, Predictions, Promises She will graduate next June.
Future Progressive Subject + will be + present participle (-ing) Ongoing future actions, Polite inquiries This time tomorrow, I will be climbing Mount Fuji.
